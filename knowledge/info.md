# ALICE: Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed

## What is ALICE?

ALICE stands for **Asset Limited, Income Constrained, Employed**. ALICE represents households that earn more than the Federal Poverty Level but less than the basic cost of living for their county. These are working families and individuals who struggle to afford basic necessities despite being employed.

## The ALICE Population

ALICE households include essential workers such as:
- Early education workers and childcare providers
- Home health aides and nursing assistants
- Retail workers and cashiers
- Food service workers
- Office assistants and administrative staff
- Truck drivers and delivery workers
- Laborers and maintenance workers

These are the backbone of our communities—hardworking people who contribute to the economy but live paycheck to paycheck, constantly at risk of financial crisis.

## The ALICE Threshold (Household Survival Budget)

### What is the ALICE Threshold?

The **ALICE Threshold**, also called the Household Survival Budget, is the minimum income a household needs to afford basic necessities in a specific county without public or private assistance.

### What the ALICE Threshold Includes:

The threshold is calculated based on the actual costs of these essential items:

1. **Housing** - Rent or mortgage payments
2. **Childcare** - Cost of care for young children (if applicable)
3. **Food** - Groceries and basic nutrition
4. **Transportation** - Vehicle costs, insurance, gas, or public transit
5. **Healthcare** - Insurance premiums, out-of-pocket medical costs
6. **Technology** - Basic phone and internet service (essential for work and school)
7. **Taxes** - Federal, state, and local taxes
8. **Miscellaneous** - Other basic necessities

### How the ALICE Threshold Varies:

The ALICE Threshold is **NOT a one-size-fits-all number**. It varies by:
- **County**: Costs differ significantly across Arkansas
- **Household size**: Number of adults and children
- **Ages of children**: Infant care costs more than school-age care
- **Year**: Costs increase over time

### Example ALICE Thresholds in Arkansas (2023):

- **Single Adult**: Approximately $30,000-$35,000 annually (varies by county)
- **Single Parent with Infant**: Approximately $50,000-$60,000 annually
- **Family of Four** (2 adults, 1 preschooler, 1 school-age child): Approximately $60,000-$75,000 annually
- **Two Adults**: Approximately $45,000-$50,000 annually

*Note: Exact amounts vary significantly by county due to differences in housing costs, childcare availability, and other factors.*

## The ALICE Stability Budget (Household Stability Budget)

### What is the ALICE Stability Budget?

The **ALICE Stability Budget**, also called the **Household Stability Budget**, estimates the income a household needs not just to *survive*, but to be financially *stable* over the long term. It is **higher than the Household Survival Budget (the ALICE Threshold)**.

While the Survival Budget covers only the bare minimum to live and work today, the Stability Budget adds the cost of:

1. **Savings** - A savings category (often estimated at about 10% of the budget) to handle emergencies and build assets
2. **More realistic essentials** - Sustainable costs for housing, childcare, healthcare, and other necessities
3. **Long-term needs** - Modest room for planning ahead rather than living paycheck to paycheck

### Survival Budget vs. Stability Budget

- **Household Survival Budget (ALICE Threshold)**: The bare-minimum cost of basic necessities - housing, childcare, food, transportation, healthcare, technology, and taxes. A household below this is counted as ALICE or in poverty.
- **Household Stability Budget**: The cost of a sustainable, financially secure household, including savings. It is meaningfully above the Survival Budget.

A household can be **above** the ALICE Threshold (so not counted as ALICE) yet still fall **below** the Stability Budget - meaning they can cover today's costs but have little cushion against a financial shock.

### Why the Stability Budget Matters

The Stability Budget shows that simply rising above the ALICE Threshold does not mean a family is financially secure. It highlights the gap between *getting by* and *getting ahead*, and helps set goals for wages and policies that move households toward lasting stability.

## The Federal Poverty Level (FPL)

### What is the Federal Poverty Level?

The **Federal Poverty Level (FPL)** or **Federal Poverty Line** is the official measure used by the U.S. government to determine eligibility for many assistance programs. It's a national standard that does NOT account for:
- Geographic cost differences
- Actual cost of living
- Childcare needs
- Healthcare costs beyond basic food and shelter

### Arkansas Federal Poverty Level (2023):

- **Individual**: $14,580/year
- **Family of 2**: $19,720/year
- **Family of 3**: $24,860/year
- **Family of 4**: $30,000/year

### Why the FPL is Different from ALICE Threshold:

The Federal Poverty Level was created in the 1960s and is based on an outdated formula that:
1. Only considers food costs (multiplied by 3)
2. Doesn't account for regional cost differences
3. Ignores childcare, transportation, and healthcare costs
4. Hasn't kept pace with actual living costs

**The ALICE Threshold is a more accurate measure** of what families actually need to survive in today's economy in specific locations.

## ALICE in Arkansas

### Statewide Statistics (2023):

- **28% of Arkansas households are ALICE** (approximately 537,094 households)
- **13% live below the Federal Poverty Level**
- **Combined: 41% of Arkansas households** struggle to afford basic necessities
- This means **only 59% of households** can afford the basics without assistance

### What This Means:

Nearly **half of all Arkansas families** are either in poverty or working but unable to afford basic needs. These aren't people who refuse to work—they ARE working, often multiple jobs, but wages haven't kept pace with the rising cost of living.

## Why ALICE Matters

Understanding ALICE is crucial because it:

1. **Reveals Hidden Struggles**: Many ALICE households don't qualify for assistance because they earn "too much," yet they still can't make ends meet.

2. **Informs Better Policies**: Accurate data on the true cost of living helps create policies that support working families.

3. **Highlights Economic Vulnerability**: ALICE families are one crisis away from poverty—a car repair, medical emergency, or job loss can push them over the edge.

4. **Shows the Reality of Work**: Having a job doesn't guarantee financial stability in today's economy.

## Common ALICE Questions

### "How is someone calculated as ALICE?"

A household is ALICE if their income is:
- **Above** the Federal Poverty Level
- **Below** the ALICE Threshold (Household Survival Budget) for their county and household composition

### "Is ALICE the same across Arkansas?"

No. The ALICE Threshold varies by county because:
- Housing costs differ (rural vs. urban areas)
- Childcare availability and costs vary
- Transportation needs differ
- Local cost of living varies significantly

### "What's the difference between poverty and ALICE?"

- **Poverty**: Income below the Federal Poverty Level (~$30,000 for a family of 4)
- **ALICE**: Income above poverty but below the survival budget (~$30,000-$75,000 for a family of 4 in Arkansas, depending on county)

Both groups struggle financially, but ALICE households often don't qualify for assistance programs despite being unable to afford basic needs.
